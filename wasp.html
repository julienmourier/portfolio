Case 1: Sprogø Island

Importing 5 years of wind data time-series into the WAsP Climate Analyst, to create an Observed Wind Climate (OWC).

In the next video, we import in WAsP the OWC, and vector maps of the elevation and roughness.

Based on the location of the met mast and its surroundings, we can calculate a Generalised Wind Climate (GWC).

Then, based on the GWC and the topography, we can estimate a Predicted Wind Climate (PWC) for any future wind turbine location.

Finally, based on the power curve of our wind turbine model, we estimate the Annual Energy Production (AEP).

Case 6: Comparison of linear vs. CFD model

When the terrain is very steep and the wind enters turbulent flow, the linear model becomes less precise. For a fee, WAsP allows us to simulate with a Computational Fluid Dynamics (CFD) model.

Case 6: Comparison of linear vs. CFD model - Step 1/9

A met mast has collected wind data for this project. Around it, we can see that the elevation goes quickly from 180 to 700m.

Case 6: Comparison of linear vs. CFD model - Step 2/9

Similarly, there is a rapid change of elevation around the future wind turbines.

Case 6: Comparison of linear vs. CFD model - Step 3/9

The RIX (ruggedness index) definitely indicates a steep terrain around the met mast.

Case 6: Comparison of linear vs. CFD model - Step 4/9

The RIX (ruggedness index) also indicates a steep terrain around the turbines.

Case 6: Comparison of linear vs. CFD model - Step 5/9

After simulation, we see a clear distinction between the IBZ (linear) model and the CFD model regarding the wind speed.

Case 6: Comparison of linear vs. CFD model - Step 6/9

We also see quite a difference regarding the wind power density between the two models.

Case 6: Comparison of linear vs. CFD model - Step 7/9

From the measured/observed wind data of the met mast, the effects of the terrain is removed to create a Generalised Wind Climate. Because the wind behaves differently in a complex terrain, we get different results if we use the IBZ or the CFD model.

Case 6: Comparison of linear vs. CFD model - Step 8/9

Using the generated GWC, the model will then take the effects of the terrain around the turbines into account to predict the wind. The two models simulate the effects of terrain differently (laminar flow vs turbulent flow) and so the results are different.

Case 6: Comparison of linear vs. CFD model - Step 9/9

Similarly, the potential wind energy calculated is also quite different between the two models.
