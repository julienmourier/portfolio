Case 1: Royalla Solar Farm

This is the most complete study, going through many simulation steps and trying out the different tools and options.

The use case is a 24 MWp (20 MWac) PV plant with fixed orientation, near Canberra.

    Royalla - Detailed losses

Royalla - Detailed losses - Step 1/31

The first parameter to change in the detailed losses is the thermal factor. We select "free mounted modules", which has a better ventilation than a roof (integrated) system.

Royalla - Detailed losses - Step 2/31

This has immediate implication. With a lower temperature, the voltage will be higher. We expect more power output, which also means the inverter will reach their max. more often. Thus, we see a higher overload loss from inv. power limitation (1.3%).

Royalla - Detailed losses - Step 3/31

Just with the thermal change, the simulation result is much better with 4487 MWh/year compare to 4335 earlier.

Royalla - Detailed losses - Step 4/31

We see that the system is often at maximum capacity. This is a trade-off, we could opt for bigger inverters (more expensive) or accept some loss.

Royalla - Detailed losses - Step 5/31

Meanwhile, with the correct thermal factor, the array no longer reaches the min. voltage.

Royalla - Detailed losses - Step 6/31

The "Mismatch loss" is mainly due to the fact that the characteristics of each module are never rigorously identical. For our 2.4 MWp block, we have 408 strings divided amongst 8 MPPTs, so 51 strings on each MPPT input, and 20 modules on each string. This tool, based on the module characteristics, randomizes a number of module configurations and gives us the avg. mismatch loss.

Royalla - Detailed losses - Step 7/31

First, we see the mismatch within a string (module mismatch), mainly due to a mistmatch of currents.

Royalla - Detailed losses - Step 8/31

Then we see the mistmatch between strings (string mismatch), mainly due to a mistmatch of voltages.

Royalla - Detailed losses - Step 9/31

We can go deeper into the study of the mismatch in voltage between strings, first by defining how the strings are aranged on one table.

Royalla - Detailed losses - Step 10/31

And then by defining the whole layout. We get the mismatch loss and other results for each inverter (MPPT) input, taking the length of the wires into consideration. Here for MPPT #1.

Royalla - Detailed losses - Step 11/31

And here for MPPT #5, with a 4 mm² wire section.

Royalla - Detailed losses - Step 12/31

We can directly check the mismatch figure for each MPPT input, and see the mismatch changing depending on the wire section, here with 2.5 mm².

Royalla - Detailed losses - Step 13/31

And here the same figure with a 6 mm² wire section.

Royalla - Detailed losses - Step 14/31

After defining the mismatch losses, we need to estimate the DC ohmic losses, which depend on the length and section of the wires.

Royalla - Detailed losses - Step 15/31

The wider the section, the less losses. But like most things, it is a trade-off between the price of the wires and the amount of acceptable DC losses.

Royalla - Detailed losses - Step 16/31

Thanks to the previous mismatch study, we could calculate the average length of a string circuit (303m). With 4 mm², we have a total wire cost of 984 AUD, and 1.6% power loss.

Royalla - Detailed losses - Step 17/31

If it's too expensive, we can opt for 2.5 mm² wires, but then the loss is 2.6%.

Royalla - Detailed losses - Step 18/31

Or we can choose 6 mm² to minimize the loss (1.1%). 4 mm² seems like a good compromise.

Royalla - Detailed losses - Step 19/31

Moving onto the AC ohmic losses, we check the transmission line the plant is connected to.

Royalla - Detailed losses - Step 20/31

Still checking the transmission line the plant is connected to.

Royalla - Detailed losses - Step 21/31

The length of AC wires from inverter to injection point is roughly estimated at 700m. With a 1200 mm² wire section, we would get 8% loss.

Royalla - Detailed losses - Step 22/31

Here, as advised, we minimize the voltage drop to a minimum, with 15000 mm² AC wires.

Royalla - Detailed losses - Step 23/31

We can achieve a similar result with smaller/cheaper 10000 mm² wires, and an acceptable <1% loss.

Royalla - Detailed losses - Step 24/31

The soiling loss is estimated at 1.5% mainly because the PV plant is next to a busy highway (dust). Snow coverage in winter, and O&M cleaning measures can also be considered.

Royalla - Detailed losses - Step 25/31

The IAM (Incidence Angle Modifier) losses are due to the reflexion and transmission of the sun's ray at each material interface (air-glass, glass-EVA, EVA-cell). This is calculated based on the module's definitions (type of glass, and if anti-reflective coating).

Royalla - Detailed losses - Step 26/31

The auxiliaries losses can be estimated based on the inverter's standby and cooling behavior (e.g. fans working). This information is usually found in the inverter's datasheet.

Royalla - Detailed losses - Step 27/31

With the ageing losses, we can use reliable ageing parameters of the model, to compare against the warranty worse case scenario. And because the simulation report is for a year, we can choose which year we want to simulate.

Royalla - Detailed losses - Step 28/31

At year 10, we have a 3.80% degradation factor.

Case 2: Bungala Solar Farm

This study focuses on the specificities of PV tracking systems.

Bungala SF - Step 1/24

Knowing the minimum temperature of a new site is essential to estimate the maximum voltage the PV array can reach.

Bungala SF - Step 2/24

In the hourly values, the minimum temperature reached over the course of a year is also -1°C.

Bungala SF - Step 3/24

In Europe, the default "lower temperature for absolute voltage limit" is -10°C.

Bungala SF - Step 4/24

We used -6°C for Royalla (Canberra). For Bungala Solar Farm (South Australia), it seems adequate to use -1°C.

Bungala SF - Step 5/24

However, after defining the modules and inverters actually used in this project, we see that the strings could potentially reach a max. voltage of 1521 V, with the inverter max. input being 1500 V.

Bungala SF - Step 6/24

In order for these parameters to be acceptable, we have to define the lowest temperature as 4°C, in which case the voltage can't exceed 1500 V. It is still extremely rare, it would mean the array being at maximum power (so max. irradiation) while at the lowest temperature. The system usually warms up as the sun rises. It is a safety value though.

Bungala SF - Step 7/24

For our tracking sytem study, we will try backtracking alone, irradiance optimization alone, combination of the two, and simple tracking.

Bungala SF - Step 8/24

We will simulate a standard 5MW block, as highlighted on the satellite view.

Bungala SF - Step 9/24

With the "simple tracking", the system just tracks the course of the sun. We get shading loss (irradiance and electrical) as the system doesn't pay attention to the shades it's casting on the other rows.

Bungala SF - Step 10/24

The "optimization tracking" typically gets in the position where it is receiving the most irradiation, which can be horizontal if the sky is clouded or the sun not yet on the horizon. As such, we get a better total irradiation, but still shading losses.

Bungala SF - Step 11/24

The "backtracking" makes sure the PV rows never cast shadows on one another. As such, the shading loss is minimal, but the modules don't face perfectly the sun.

Bungala SF - Step 12/24

With the added "irradiance optimization", we get an improvement on the total irradiation, but the priority is still no shadow, so the modules are still back-tracking from where the optimal irradiation could be. For a 1P (1 module in portrait) system, this is the best configuration though, and the one mostly used.

Bungala SF - Step 13/24

However, the shading in 1P is so impactful because no string, and no submodule is spared. In 1L (1 module in landscape) configuration, only 1 ouf of 3 submodules is shaded, which is shunted by its bypass diode, so we still have 2 fully functional submodules per module. The shape of 2L is still the same as 1P configuration.

Bungala SF - Step 14/24

Finally, with a 4L configuration, we only get 1/4th of the modules shaded. So we can actually focus on the irradiance optimization because the shading is not as impactful.

Bungala SF - Step 15/24

With a 4L configuration, the tables are much wider, so we increase the pitch accordingly, but at the end, the footprint is stil the same per number of modules. It must be noted that the mounting structure for a 4L would be more complex.

Bungala SF - Step 16/24

This configuration gives us the maximum irradiance (2486 kWh/m²). And although we have shading irr. loss is at 6%, the shading elec. loss is seriously minimized (only -1%). However, this loss is still enough to make the 1P with backtracking + irradiance optimization the winner, but not by much (11.324 vs 11.262 GWh). In reality, depending on the latitude of the site, 1P backtracking solution are not systematically the best.

Bungala SF - Step 17/24

Simulation report for the full PV plant (276 GWh).

Real production in 2019 at 272 GWh, very close to our simulation.

Adding a grid limitation at 110 MW.

As seen in steps 5 and 6, the Pnom ratio was 1.01, i.e. having 2500 kW of inverter for 2500 kWp of modules. The inverters aren't not working at full power most of the time, so unless they were a bargain, it could have been wise to use smaller/cheaper inverters. Residential PV - Step 7/24

Power optimizers (not to confuse with micro-inverters) are DC-DC converters and quite complex to simulate. SolarEdge products are leading the market. They can be efficient on small installations where each module is optimized.

Residential PV - Step 8/24

Parameters of a single optimizer. Its input voltage range is quite wide (13-87V). It performs MPP tracking on each module, adapting the voltage to get the max. power out of it. But the output is still DC. Essentially, in power electronics, this is a buck-boost converter.

Residential PV - Step 9/24

SolarEdge Optimizer I/V curve, with a 500W module.

Residential PV - Step 10/24

SolarEdge Optimizer I/V curve, with a 300W module. Essentially it performs MPP on the input, adapting the voltage to get the max. current and power. And independently, it can return this power with a different output voltage, which will be conditioned by the number of modules in series and the SolarEdge inverter input voltage. That inverter will be highly efficient because its only task is to transform from DC to AC, from a fixed voltage.

Residential PV - Step 11/24

SolarEdge Optimizer P/V curve. We can see more easily that the optimizer is capable of delivering the same power across a wide voltage range.

Residential PV - Step 12/24

Some PVsyst documentation about power optimizers.

Residential PV - Step 13/24

With 6x DualSun 500W modules and their corresponding P505 optimizers, we must choose a corresponding SolarEdge inverter, here the SE3000H.

Residential PV - Step 14/24

Comparison between SMA (single inverter) and SolarEdge (with optimizers). The optimizers have an conversion efficiency, so unfortunately we lose 2.7% there. But then, we have 0% of loss due to module mistmatch. And we have a very good inverter efficiency with only 1.1% loss. The SMA inverter has an average loss of 4.7% during all conditions of operation. In the end, the production is better with the optimizers, but the system also more costly.

Residential PV - Step 15/24

Similarly, micro-inverters are also more and more popular for small installations, to get the most out of each module. Here, we also have an MPP tracking on each module, but then it is directly converted to AC. The only issue is that, with a "low" voltage of 230Vac instead of 500-800Vdc, the AC ohmic loss can increase exponentially with the length of cable. It is still okay for small installations though. The advantage compared to power optimizers is that we save the cost of having a main inverter.

Residential PV - Step 16/24

Choosing the right model of micro-inverter to the PV module.

Residential PV - Step 17/24

Comparison of the 3 different systems. The micro-inverters have an efficiency of 96.6% (3.4% loss). Overall, the results between the system with power optimizers and the one with micro-inverters are very similar. The decision should be based on the cost of installation.

Residential PV - Step 18/24

PVsyst can also simulate grid-connected systems with self-consumption, meaning the energy is used when there is a need (load), or else is injected onto the grid. Inversely, it can consume from the grid when the PV system doesn't provide enough.

Storage and off-grid - Step 3/21

First off, the "detailed pre-sizing" part helps us to make out the main characteristics of our storage system, namely the PLOL (Probability of Loss of Load), the autonomy in days, and the battery voltage, mainly based on our consumption needs and size of our appliances.

Storage and off-grid - Step 4/21

In the battery database, each model has its own characteristics. We find both lead-acid and lithium-ion technology.

Storage and off-grid - Step 5/21

Many phenomenons must be integrated to reliably model a battery: such as the voltage according to the State Of Charge (SOC), the effect of temperature on the battery capacity, the self-discharge, the degradation over time, etc.

Storage and off-grid - Step 6/21

Battery cell voltage as a function of the SOC, for different charge and discharge currents.

Storage and off-grid - Step 7/21

Once the storage is defined to our needs, we can choose our PV modules, and also the type of solar charge controller. There are many different types.

Storage and off-grid - Step 8/21

Some of the main functions of the controller include stopping the charging when the battery is full (95%), and re-enabling it below a threshold (75%), and also stopping the discharge (loss of power) when the battery is too low to avoid damaging it.

Grid-connected systems can also have a battery storage, with a different battery strategy. We might want to increase self-consumption, as studied from step 18 in the residential case.

Storage and off-grid - Step 16/21

Weak grid islanding strategy: in some countries, the grid is highly unreliable. So for critical industries, the storage can just be to cover these small random grid cuts. To simulate this, we can provide a randomized grid unavailability profile, where we can give the average duration and number of grid cuts. From there, we can size our battery storage.

Storage and off-grid - Step 17/21

Another strategy can be for peak shaving. We store the energy to release it during the known peak consumption hours. In this scenario, we limit our 22.3 MWac PV plant at 16MW, so we store the excess energy, and we release it at a max. discharge of 2.5MW starting at 5pm (evening peak load).
